Above Iceland

Six entries from the lava fields to the aurora highlands.

Entry 01 of 06

First Views: Reykjanes Peninsula

February 7, 10:00 PM

The DJI lifted off just as the last light surrendered to the Arctic dark. Below, the lava fields of Reykjanes stretched toward the sea — ancient flows frozen mid-pour, now dusted with snow. Iceland from the air looks like another planet: familiar enough to recognize as earth, alien enough to make you question it.

Entry 02 of 06

Where Lava Meets the Sea

February 7, 10:03 PM

At the peninsula's edge, the Atlantic crashes against columns of black basalt. From altitude, the foam looks like white paint brushed over dark canvas. The wind hit 40 knots. The drone fought back.

Entry 03 of 06

Chasing Green

February 8, 12:43 AM

We drove three hours into the highlands, away from any light. When the forecast finally delivered, the sky opened. Flying in aurora light requires trust — the sensors read the landscape in infrared, blind to what your eyes see. Green curtains hung from the stars.

Entry 04 of 06

Silence at Altitude

February 8, 12:46 AM

There is a specific silence that comes at 120 meters above Iceland's interior plateau. No trees to break the wind. No roads, no fences. Just the drone's hum and the magnetic storm burning overhead.

Entry 05 of 06

The Full Curtain

February 8, 12:47 AM

This was the peak. KP index 6. The entire northern sky was moving — greens folding into purples, structures rippling like a flag in slow motion. I nearly forgot to fly the shot.

Entry 06 of 06

Coming Down

February 8, 1:47 AM

The aurora faded around 2 AM. We stayed until the battery died on the drone, then sat in the dark for another hour. The kind of night that makes the flight home feel distant.
